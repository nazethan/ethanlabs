### **Aim**
> To study the detailed technical specifications, characteristics, and performance metrics of various computer network cables and their respective connectors.

### **Apparatus Required**
- Detailed diagrams or color prints of networking cables and connectors
- Specification data sheets for standard network media
- Samples of Twisted Pair (Cat5e/Cat6), Coaxial (RG-6/RG-59), and Fiber Optic (SMF/MMF) cables (if available for reference)

### **Theory**
#### **1. Twisted Pair Cables (UTP & STP)**
- Twisted pair wiring consists of pairs of insulated, solid copper wires twisted around each other. This twisting creates a magnetic shield effect that significantly reduces crosstalk (interference between adjacent wire pairs) and external Electromagnetic Interference (EMI). Unshielded Twisted Pair (UTP) is standard, relying solely on the twisting for protection. Shielded Twisted Pair (STP) wraps the pairs in metallic foil or braided mesh to provide robust protection in high-EMI environments like factories. Modern cables (like Cat6) also include a central plastic spline to physically separate the four pairs, further reducing internal crosstalk.

- **Specifications:**
  - **Wire Gauge:** Typically 22 to 24 AWG (American Wire Gauge).
  - **Cat5e:** Supports speeds up to 1 Gbps (Gigabit Ethernet), maximum frequency of 100 MHz, maximum distance of 100 meters.
  - **Cat6:** Supports speeds up to 10 Gbps (up to 55 meters), maximum frequency of 250 MHz, standard 1 Gbps up to 100 meters.
  - **Cat6a:** Supports speeds of 10 Gbps up to the full 100 meters, maximum frequency of 500 MHz.

- The RJ-45 (Registered Jack 45) is an 8-Position 8-Contact (8P8C) modular connector. The internal pins are usually gold-plated (e.g., 50-micron gold plating) to prevent corrosion and ensure maximum conductivity. They are wired according to specific color-code standards, primarily T568A or T568B. A Straight-Through cable uses the same standard on both ends, while a Crossover cable uses T568A on one end and T568B on the other.

**2. Coaxial Cables**
- Coaxial cable is designed to transmit high-frequency electrical signals with low loss. It features a concentric topological design: a central solid copper or copper-clad steel core conductor, surrounded by an insulating dielectric foam or solid Teflon. This is encased in a tubular conducting shield (usually a woven copper braid or aluminum foil) that serves as the ground and protects against EMI, all wrapped in a PVC outer jacket. Because the electromagnetic field carrying the signal exists entirely in the space between the inner and outer conductors, coaxial cables can be installed next to metal objects without power loss.

- **Specifications:**
  - Impedance: Typically 50 Ohms (used for digital data/legacy Ethernet like RG-58) or 75 Ohms (used for broadband, cable TV, and video like RG-6 and RG-59).
  - RG-59: Thinner core, higher attenuation, best for short runs (e.g., analog video). 75 Ohms.
  - RG-6: Thicker core, heavier shielding, lower attenuation, ideal for high-frequency, long-distance broadband internet drops. 75 Ohms.

- The BNC connector features a bayonet-style, twist-and-lock mechanism ensuring a tight, secure connection that won't accidentally pull out, commonly rated for frequencies up to 4 GHz. The F-Type connector uses a threaded screw-on mechanism and ingeniously utilizes the solid central conductor of the coaxial cable itself as the connector's central pin, minimizing signal loss for broadband applications.

#### **3. Fiber Optic Cables**
- Fiber optics represent a fundamental shift from electrical signaling to optical (light) signaling. The cable consists of an ultra-pure glass or plastic core, surrounded by a cladding layer with a slightly lower refractive index. This difference in refractive index causes "total internal reflection," trapping the light pulses inside the core as they travel. They are immune to EMI, RFI, and lightning strikes. Single-Mode Fiber (SMF) has a tiny core that allows only one pathway (mode) of light to travel, usually generated by a laser. Multi-Mode Fiber (MMF) has a larger core allowing multiple light modes to travel simultaneously, usually generated by LEDs or VCSELs.

- **Specifications:**
  - Core/Cladding Diameter: SMF is typically 8 to 10 microns (core) / 125 microns (cladding). MMF is typically 50 or 62.5 microns (core) / 125 microns (cladding).
  - Bandwidth & Distance: SMF provides theoretically unlimited bandwidth over massive distances (40 km to over 100 km without repeating). MMF supports 10 Gbps to 100 Gbps but is limited to shorter campus-level distances (300 meters to 550 meters) due to modal dispersion (light rays spreading out and blurring over distance).
  - Wavelengths: Commonly operates at 850 nm, 1300 nm, 1310 nm, or 1550 nm.

- Fiber connectors demand extreme precision; a misalignment of a fraction of a micron causes massive signal loss (insertion loss).
  - **SC (Subscriber Connector):** Features a rigid push-pull locking mechanism and a 2.5mm ferrule (the ceramic tube holding the fiber).
  - **LC (Lucent Connector):** A small-form-factor connector with a snap-in retaining latch and a smaller 1.25mm ferrule, designed for high-density switches and data centers.
  - **ST (Straight Tip):** Uses a bayonet twist-lock mechanism similar to a BNC connector with a 2.5mm ferrule.
